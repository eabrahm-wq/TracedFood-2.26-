What We Found

The electrolyte category has a problem that protein doesn't: the core science is genuinely simple and genuinely settled, but the marketing infrastructure built around it implies complexity that justifies premium pricing. Sodium, potassium, and magnesium in appropriate ratios replace what sweat removes. That's been known since at least the 1970s WHO cholera research. What the category sells is a branded version of that knowledge â€” and the brands differ mainly in which aspect of the science they emphasize and whether their financial relationships with the people presenting it to you are disclosed.

The sodium dosing debate is the clearest example. LMNT's 1,000mg sodium is legitimate for its stated use case â€” endurance athletes, heavy sweaters, people on ketogenic diets that increase sodium excretion. The problem is that this specific use case was communicated to a mass audience through podcast sponsorships where the economic relationships between brand and host were either undisclosed or incompletely disclosed. That changes how the "evidence" reads.

Liquid IV's CTT is the most flagrant example of trademarking public domain science. Oral rehydration therapy using a sodium-glucose cotransport mechanism is how every pediatric rehydration packet works â€” including Pedialyte since 1966 and DripDrop since 2010. Liquid IV did not invent it. They named it, branded it, and sold it to Unilever for a reported $500M. DripDrop uses the same mechanism and explains it plainly on their FAQ page. The science is identical; the transparency is not.

Pedialyte is the category's most interesting case study in use-case drift. A clinical formula that's been working for 60 years â€” unchanged, validated, legitimate â€” repositioned for adult hangovers through influencer seeding (#TeamPedialyte, beer koozies, festival sampling) without a new FDA indication and without clinical evidence of superiority to water for hangover beyond basic rehydration. The product didn't change. The marketing premise did.

How to Read This Category
  • Sodium dose matters and is use-case specific. 55mg (Ultima) is appropriate for people already getting sodium from food who want casual hydration. 300â€“370mg (Nuun, DripDrop, Pedialyte) aligns with moderate exercise. 1,000mg (LMNT) is appropriate for endurance exercise or keto. Neither is the "correct" daily dose for everyone.
  • CTT, Cellular Transport Technology, Hydration Multiplier â€” these are marketing names for the sodium-glucose cotransport mechanism described in WHO oral rehydration guidelines since the 1970s. The mechanism is real. The trademark is not science. DripDrop uses the same mechanism and calls it ORS science. Pedialyte has used it since 1966 and calls it what it is.
  • Sugar in ORS-based products is functional, not incidental. A sugar-free version of Liquid IV cannot deliver the same absorption speed the CTT claim implies â€” their own science acknowledges this. DripDrop's zero-sugar version uses a different transport mechanism (sodium-amino acid co-transport) and is transparent about the difference.
  • Influencer economics in this category are systematically underreported. The FTC requires disclosure of material connections â€” but enforcement is weak and compliance is inconsistent. Pedialyte's #TeamPedialyte program sent free merchandise in exchange for posts. Treat undisclosed sponsorships involving nutrition claims as a transparency flag regardless of which direction the relationship runs.
  • Conglomerate ownership creates cost-pressure incentives. NestlÃ© owns Nuun, Unilever owns Liquid IV, Abbott has owned Pedialyte since 1966. DripDrop is the only top-tier brand in this category that has never been acquired. That's unusual. It doesn't make the others untrustworthy â€” but watch for reformulations and certifications that lapse post-acquisition.
  • NSF Certified for Sport â‰  formula quality. NSF sport cert confirms that what's listed on the label is present and no banned substances are included. It does not evaluate sodium adequacy, ingredient quality, or whether the science claims are accurate. DripDrop (16oz sticks) has it. Nuun, Pedialyte, Liquid IV, Ultima, and LMNT do not.
  • ROAR Organic introduced a new pattern: lifestyle brand with trace electrolytes. ~80mg sodium per 18oz bottle is not an electrolyte product by any clinical threshold. It's a flavored organic water with B vitamins. "Complete Hydration" on the label is marketing for everyday casual consumption â€” but the branding reads like athletic recovery. USDA Organic is real.
